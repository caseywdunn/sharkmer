reads, and at k=21 and k=31, were used to separate coverage failures
from graph traversal failures.

### AT-rich kmer coverage gaps

The Drosophila 16S and Heliconius ND4 failures are caused by AT-rich
regions within the amplicon where kmer coverage drops below the minimum
threshold, fragmenting the de Bruijn graph into disconnected forward and
backward components.

This is not a gap in the *sequence data* — the reads span the region
continuously. It is a gap in *kmer coverage*: in AT-rich stretches, the
low sequence complexity means fewer distinct kmers exist, so the count
per unique kmer should in principle be higher. However, at the same time
AT-rich kmers from elsewhere in the (AT-rich) mitochondrial genome also
contribute the same or similar kmers, fragmenting the signal. The net
effect is that individual kmer counts in the amplicon's AT-rich region
fall below min_count=2 at the available read depth.

**Why smaller k helps:** Shorter kmers have higher coverage per unique
kmer (more reads share the same kmer when there are fewer possible
kmers). For Heliconius ND4, the AT-rich region has kmers with count=1
at k=31 but count >= 2 at k=21, bridging the gap. For Drosophila 16S,
the AT content is more extreme (83-90%) and even 21-mers remain
undercovered. At k=15, the graph explodes with branching (0.92 ratio)
because the kmers are too short to be specific.

**Graph evidence (k=31, 8M reads, exact primers):**

- Drosophila 16S: 229 nodes total. Forward component (146 nodes)
  terminates at `...CCCCAATAAAATATT` (83% AT). Backward component (83
  nodes) starts at `TTTTGACTAAAAAATAAAA...` (90% AT). Zero overlap.
- Heliconius ND4: 202 nodes total. Forward component terminates after
  just 1 node (the start kmer itself). Backward component has 166
  nodes. The forward primer's flanking region is deeply AT-rich and
  no 31-mer extending from it has count >= 2.

### Failure categories at 1M reads

The perfect-match failures fall into three categories:

1. **Insufficient read coverage** (Porites 16S, Xenia CO1, Drosophila
   ND4): Large genome dilutes mt reads. More reads solve the problem —
   Drosophila ND4 recovers at 4M with exact primers.

2. **AT-rich kmer coverage gaps** (Heliconius ND4, Drosophila 16S):
   Graph fragments in AT-rich amplicon regions where per-kmer counts
   fall below threshold. Smaller k helps (Heliconius ND4 recovers at
   k=21, 8M reads). Drosophila 16S is the hardest case — fails at all
   tested k values. Would require variable-k assembly or longer reads.

3. **Structural complexity** (Rhopilema ITS, Drosophila 28S/ITS):
   rDNA copy variation, retrotransposon insertions, or very large
   amplicons create graph complexity beyond the current traversal
   limits.
